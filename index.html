02 — A lineage, not a single leap

The models that built the foundation

From a single paper proving pretraining works, to a router deciding how hard to think — the foundation model era in nine stops.

2018
BERT · Google

Reading in both directions

Introduced bidirectional context, letting a model read a whole sentence at once instead of left-to-right — a breakthrough for understanding-heavy tasks like search and classification.

2018
GPT‑1 · OpenAI

Proof that pretraining transfers

Showed that a model pretrained on unlabeled text could be fine-tuned for many downstream tasks — the seed idea behind every foundation model that followed.

2019
GPT‑2 · OpenAI

Coherent text, and a staged release

Scaled up to 1.5B parameters and generated long, fluent passages good enough that OpenAI staggered its release over misuse concerns — the first time model capability itself became a policy question.

2020
GPT‑3 · OpenAI

Few-shot learning at scale

175B parameters and the ability to perform new tasks from just a few examples in the prompt, with no retraining. This is the release that made "foundation model" a useful term.

2022
PaLM · Google

Reasoning at 540B parameters

Pushed scale further and showed strong multi-step reasoning and multilingual performance, reinforcing that capability kept climbing with size and data.

Nov 2022
ChatGPT (GPT‑3.5) · OpenAI

The moment it left the lab

Conversational fine-tuning turned a research model into a product anyone could use in a browser — the fastest-adopted consumer app in history at the time.

Feb 2023
LLaMA · Meta

Opening the weights

Smaller, more efficient models released for research use sparked an open-source ecosystem of fine-tunes and local deployments outside the big labs.

Mar 2023
GPT‑4 · OpenAI

Multimodal reasoning

Added image understanding alongside text and a marked jump in reliability on exams, logic, and long-context tasks.

2025
GPT‑5 · OpenAI

A system, not a single model

Unified a fast responder and a slower "thinking" model behind a real-time router — the current shape of the frontier: knowing when to answer instantly and when to reason longer.

04 — What the field's own writing says

Reading the literature

Three pieces, read back to back, sketch the shape of the argument around foundation models — what they are, why they matter, and how they actually work under the hood.

01  "What Are Foundation Models?" — Stanford CRFM

Coined the term itself: a single model trained on broad, largely unlabeled data at massive scale, adaptable to many downstream tasks through fine-tuning or prompting. The piece is as much a warning as a definition — centralizing so much capability into one base model means its flaws and biases inherit downstream into everything built on top of it.

02  "The Age of Generative AI" — OpenAI Blog

Frames generative AI as a general-purpose shift on the scale of earlier computing transitions — touching creative work, scientific research, and everyday productivity at once. It argues the technology should be scaled deliberately, with safety and access treated as part of the product, not an afterthought.

03  "How Does ChatGPT Work?" — Stephen Wolfram

Strips the mystique away: the model is repeatedly predicting the most statistically plausible next word, one token at a time, using a neural network shaped by the transformer architecture. What's surprising isn't the mechanism, Wolfram argues, but that something this simple, run at this scale, produces language that reads as coherent thought.

a
What separates a foundation model from a traditional NLP model?

A traditional NLP model is usually trained from scratch for one task on labeled data. A foundation model is pretrained once on broad, largely unlabeled data at massive scale, then adapted — through fine-tuning or just a good prompt — to many different tasks it was never explicitly trained for.

b
Where does GenAI show up inside an AI discipline itself?

Most visibly in code generation and debugging assistance, but also in accelerating literature reviews, generating synthetic training data, and prototyping model architectures faster than a solo researcher could by hand.

c
What ethical or societal concerns come with it?

Hallucinated but confident-sounding answers, bias inherited from training data at internet scale, unclear copyright status of generated work, job displacement in content and support roles, and a concentration of the compute and capital needed to train these models in a handful of organizations.

The through-line

Every stop on this page traces back to the same idea: a model that learns the general shape of language and image, once, at scale — and then gets pointed at whatever task shows up next. That reusability is the whole reason a research artifact turned into infrastructure this fast.
